# do research to validate the idea of user grow strategy with referal code and discount by inviting their friends and people

Yes – there’s strong evidence that “invite friends + referral code/discount” is one of the most effective and cheapest growth levers you can add, and it fits your citizenship‑prep niche very well if you design the incentives and timing correctly.[^1][^2]

## Evidence that referral programs drive growth

- Referral marketing is consistently rated as one of the most effective acquisition channels: organisations that use “refer a friend” report it as their highest‑performing tactic for new members.[^3]
- Structured incentives (discounts, rewards etc.) can boost referral participation rates by more than 300% versus having no incentive at all.[^1]
- Trust and quality are higher:
    - 92% of people trust recommendations from friends/family more than any ads.[^2]
    - Referred customers have ~37% higher retention and ~16% higher lifetime value than non‑referred customers.[^2]

Famous examples:

- Dropbox grew from 100k to around 4M users in 15 months (≈3900% growth), with ~35% of daily signups coming from referrals; the program cut CAC by ~60% vs paid ads and permanently increased signups by about 60%.[^4][^5][^2]
- Case studies across industries show referral campaigns can lift sign‑ups by 40–50%+ when properly integrated into product flows and communications.[^6]

For you, this is even more powerful because your users are in tight, trust‑heavy circles (family, same language communities, church/mosque groups, migration cohorts) where recommendations travel fast and are taken seriously.

## What kind of referral incentives work best

Across SaaS/consumer products, several patterns stand out:

- **Two‑sided rewards win:** Dropbox’s success came from giving *both* referrer and friend something valuable (extra storage). Guides on high‑performing refer‑a‑friend campaigns strongly recommend “give and get” offers (discount for friend, credit/reward for inviter).[^7][^8][^9][^4][^6]
- **Product‑aligned rewards beat generic cash:**
    - Dropbox offered more storage (core value).[^7][^4]
    - Duolingo gives free “Super” days/weeks when you invite friends who sign up and reach a small engagement milestone.[^10][^11]
    - Many brands give credits/discounts applied to the *next* purchase or subscription.[^9][^1]
- **Simple mechanics and UX:** High‑performing programs emphasise: a clear, simple explanation of “what you get,” one‑click share links, visible referral status, and rewards that trigger quickly so users feel it “works.”[^4][^6][^2]
- **Discounts/credits are a standard model:** Many SaaS/e‑commerce and membership products run offers like “Give 25% off, get 20 off yourself” because discounts are easy to understand and operationalise.[^3][^9]

All of this maps cleanly onto your model: discounts off a Sprint Pass or free extra mock tests are directly tied to your core value (passing the test).

## How to design your referral strategy for this app

Here’s how to adapt the research to your citizenship‑prep context:

### 1. Use two‑sided, exam‑aligned rewards

- For **the invited friend**:
    - X% off their first 60‑day Exam Sprint Pass, or
    - A free extra full mock test + unlock of AI explanations for 24 hours (a taste of Pro).
- For **the referrer**:
    - Extra days added to their Sprint Pass,
    - Or an extra mock‑test pack / cram‑mode unlock.

This mirrors Dropbox’s and Duolingo’s logic (reward that feels like “more of the thing I already want,” not random points).[^10][^9][^7]

### 2. Trigger referral prompts at high‑emotion moments

Use research best practice: put the referral CTA where users already feel value. For you:[^6][^2]

- After a user completes their first full mock test and sees clear, helpful feedback.
- When readiness score jumps (e.g., “You moved from 40% to 70% ready”) – show “Invite a friend who is also preparing; you each get X.”
- After they pass the real test (if you track this via a simple self‑report): “Share your success and help a friend – they get a discount on their Sprint, you get extra days.”

These are the moments when trust and willingness to share are highest.

### 3. Keep the flow dead simple

Borrow from Dropbox/other guides on mechanics:[^8][^2][^6]

- One personal referral link + a short code (for WhatsApp in particular).
- In‑app screen that shows:
    - “You have invited 0/3 friends – invite 3 friends who buy a Sprint Pass and get your next Sprint free.”
- Require a **real action** before rewarding (e.g., the friend must at least complete a diagnostic or buy a Sprint) to avoid abuse, similar to Duolingo requiring the friend to complete some lessons.[^11]


### 4. Fit incentives within your unit economics

- Examples in other industries: haircare brand Madison Reed uses “\$15 credit for me, \$15 off for friend”; many SaaS products use ~10–25 in credit/discount bands.[^9]
- In your plan you’re targeting an LTV of roughly 35–80 per paying user; designing referral rewards in the 5–15 value range per successful *paying* referral is consistent with these patterns and keeps LTV:CAC healthy.[^12][^1][^9]
- Start with caps and tiers (e.g., max 3–5 rewarded referrals per user in early phases) until you see real numbers.


### 5. Leverage cohorts and communities, not just individuals

Because your audience often studies with family or community:

- Household bundles: “Invite a family member and you both get 30% off your Sprint Pass” – makes the referral feel like a group decision.[^12]
- Community leaders: offer free Premium if a community moderator (WeChat/Facebook group admin, church/mosque rep) brings in N paying users via their code – this uses the same referral mechanics but at “whale” scale.[^6][^9]


### 6. Measure whether it’s working

Based on referral‑growth case studies:[^5][^2][^4]

Track:

- % of new signups with a referral code (aim for this to climb to 15–30% over time if the program is working).
- Free‑to‑paid conversion for referred users vs non‑referred – you should see higher conversion/retention in the referred cohort.
- Simple viral coefficient: average number of *paying* users brought in per paying user (it doesn’t have to hit 1.0; Dropbox’s famous program worked at ~0.35 when combined with other channels).[^4]
